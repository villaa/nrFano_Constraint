The Silicon Effective Fano Factor from Lindhard and Dougherty

02/21/19

A.N. Villano

Abstract:

The "effective" Fano factor for nuclear recoils is something that has been studied before both theoretically [Lind63a, Lind63b, Lind68] and experimentally [Doug91]. This factor parameterizes the variance in the ionization produced from nuclear recoils, and is strongly dependent on the recoil energy of the nuclear recoils. In order to study how new data from CDMS II, SuperCDMS, and Edelweiss may constrain the variance for germanium we have to understand the previous theoretical predictions and experimental constraints for silicon. Here I show what information on the silicon predictions and measurements I've gathered and how that information is relevant to the germanium case.

1. Lindhard energy definition


Energy Definition:

Lindhard [Lind63b] defines the dimensionless energy as follows.

\begin{equation} \epsilon = E \frac{aM_1}{Z_1 Z_2 e^2(M_1+M_2)}, \end{equation}

where $M_1$, $Z_1$, $M_2$, and $Z_2$ are the masses and atomic numbers of the projectile (subscript 1) and target (subscript 2) respectively. E is the energy of the projectile in any energy units.

From this expression, and how the variable $e$ appears, I figured out that it is in Guassian-cgs units [Gauss-cgs] where charge-squared has units of energy times distance. Since the only remaining dimensionful quantity in the expression (other than the energy) is $a$, the "radius" of the screened potential, the factor after the energy on the RHS has dimensions of inverse energy. So $\epsilon$ is dimensionless.

The value for $a$ used by Lindhard [Lind63b] is as follows.

\begin{equation} a = 0.8853 \frac{a_0}{\left(Z_1^{\frac{2}{3}} + Z_2^{\frac{2}{3}}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}}, \end{equation}

where $a_0$ is the Bohr radius. The Bohr radius in Gaussian units is simply $\hbar^2/m_e e^2$ [ Bohr-rad]. In order to not have to deal with the "Statcoulomb" unit, I simply used this expression to replace the electric charge $e$ and propagated it to the expression for $\epsilon$. If I also convert to natural units ($c = \hbar = 1$), I get this expression:

\begin{equation} \epsilon = E\cdot 0.8853\frac{a_0^2 m_e M_1}{(M_1+M_2)}\frac{1}{Z_1 Z_2 \left(Z_1^{\frac{2}{3}} + Z_2^{\frac{2}{3}}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}} , \end{equation}

Since in our analysis and many "active-detector" measurements the recoiling species is the same as the projectile species, we can make the substitution $Z \equiv Z_1 = Z_2$ and $M_1 = M_2$ to get:

\begin{equation} \epsilon = E\cdot 0.8853\frac{a_0^2 m_e}{2\sqrt{2} Z^2 Z^{\frac{1}{3}}} , \end{equation}

In natural units the Bohr radius equals 2.68$\times$10$^{-4}$ /eV (which equals 0.268 /keV) and $m_e$ is 511 keV. Also, $Z$ is 32 for germanium and 14 for silicon. That means for germanium:

\begin{equation} \epsilon_{Ge} = \mathrm{0.00353} \cdot E_{Ge} \mathrm{(keV)}, \end{equation}

and for silicon:

\begin{equation} \epsilon_{Si} = \mathrm{0.02432} \cdot E_{Si} \mathrm{(keV)}, \end{equation}

These definitions seem to match the Sorensen paper [Sor15], because $\epsilon$=0.1 corresponds to 28.3 keV and 4.11  for germanium and silicon respectively. This correspondence is confirmed by Figures 1&3 of that paper respectively.

Variance in Ionization Production and the Effective Fano Factor:

Using this non-dimensional energy, Lindhard et al. parameterize the energy deposition balance for a nuclear recoil as:

\begin{equation} \epsilon = \nu(\epsilon) + \eta(\epsilon), \end{equation}

where $\nu$ is the energy given to the lattice (phonons) and $\eta$ is the energy given to the electron system (e/h pairs). The total energy deposited is fixed and assumed to be equal to the incoming energy, but the sharing of energy can be a function of the incoming energy. This makes the standard definition of "ionization yield:" $Y=\bar{\eta}/\epsilon$, where the bar indicates the average for many identical recoils. The variance in the energy given to the electronic system is denoted (following Lindhard) as $\Omega^2$. Note that due to the constrained total energy deposit this must also be the variance of the lattice energy.

The standard Fano factor, F, is defined as follows:

\begin{equation} \sigma_N = \sqrt{F\bar{N}}, \end{equation}

where $\sigma_N$ is the standard deviation of the number of ionization pairs produced, and $\bar{N}$ is the average number of pairs produced. This equation basically is an analog to the counting-statistics variance from Poisson statistics; if the Fano factor is unity the distribution agrees with the Poisson distribution up to the second moment.

The fractional variance for the electronic energy is $\sigma_N/\bar{N}$ and in terms of Lindhard parameters this is $\Omega/\eta$. And, since $\bar{\eta} = Y\epsilon$, we can write the effective Fano factor like:

\begin{equation} \tilde{F} = \bar{N} \frac{1}{Y^2} \left(\frac{\Omega}{\epsilon}\right)^2. \end{equation}

The ratio of the variance in ionization production to the total energy of the recoil has been measured for silicon by Dougherty [Doug91]. And The ionization yield for silicon can be taken from the Lindhard paper with the parameter k=0.15. This will allow one to put Dougherty's measurements into the context of the effective Fano factor $\tilde{F}$.


2. Dougherty paper

The measurement by Dougherty [Doug91] was done by using neutrons to scatter off of silicon and looking for features in the resulting recoil spectrum. The experiment used the $^7$Li(p,n)$^7$Be reaction to produce neutrons via a proton source of around 1.8–2.7 MeV. Each proton energy produced a different maximum neutron energy. It was a "thick-target" experiment so the protons slowed to a stop in the neutron-production target and ensured that neutrons of all energies up to the maximum were produced.

The neutron scatters were observed in a silicon ionization detector with threshold of 0.5 keV ionization energy. Because of resonance structures in silicon for incoming neutron energies of 815, 566, 189, 56, and 31 keV, features are observed in the recoil spectrum. Generally the changes in elastic cross section will cause the recoil spectra to have a feature when one moves above the maximum recoil energy from a resonant-energy neutron.

Near the features in slope of the recoil energy spectra there is an inflection due to the resonant features that can be extracted by looking at a "derivative filter" of the recoil spectrum. The simple filter used is:

\begin{equation} \Delta N(k) /n \equiv \left[\sum_{i=k+1}^{k+n} N(i) - \sum_{i=k-n-1}^{k} N(i)\right]/n, \end{equation}

where $n$ was specifically chosen for each neutron energy to emphasize the inflection point.

3. Lindhard extraction and plot


In order to be sure I understood all of the details of the Dougherty paper and its relation to the Lindhard papers, I decided to re-plot the Fig. 2 of Dougherty, and overlay my own extraction of the Lindhard model for the ionization variance. First I extracted the Dougherty data (see Fig. 1 (left)) from his Fig. 2. This was straight-forward to do with "Data Thief" but it was a bit difficult because the lines for the theory models were not contiguous and had poor contrast. So I ended up tracing them with a fine-point sharpie marker and then using Data Thief [Data-Thief]. I used this figure to extract the curves shown on the Dougherty plot (see Fig. 1 (left), black solid and dashed lines).

To get the $\Omega/\epsilon$ information I used Fig. 8 of the Lindhard "Notes on Atomic Collisons III" (NACIII) paper [Lind63b]. I used the same technique of tracing them with a fine-point sharpie (red and green).

Figure 1 shows that the technique of tracing was a little unstable but I was able in the end to obtain the same graph as Dougherty and also confirm that the estimates for the theoretical $\Omega/\epsilon$ came from the Lindhard NACIII paper Fig. 8. When I converted the ratio into the effective Fano factor using the equations of Sec. 1 (and a $k$=0.15 for the Lindhard model of the silicon ionization yield) I get Figure 1 (right). The effective Fano factor gets to over 200 times larger than the standard Poisson fluctuation (Fano of one).


4. further work

This note is pretty simple and self-contained. The technique is interesting and that might be good to study more in the future, but is not relevant on the short timescale. Here are some areas of further work related to this:

  1. Try to get better specifications of the Lindhard and Dougherty curves for the theoretical predictions for the ionization variance (and therefore the effective Fano factor too).

  2. Get a double-check on the conversion from ionization variance to the effective Fano factor.


5. conclusions

I was able to extract the data from the Dougherty paper and convert it to the effective Fano factor. Furthermore, I was able to match the theoretical predictions from the Dougherty paper where he quoted the Lindhard predictions, by taking the original predictions from that paper and converting the energy scale ($\epsilon$ relations in Sec. 1) and converting to the appropriate percent scale that Dougherty used for the "excess" ionization variance.

  1. Note that the effective Fano factors are quite large for silicon near 100 keV recoils, over 200 times wider than a Poisson fluctuation. This result should be checked.
