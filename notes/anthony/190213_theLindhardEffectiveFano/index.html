The Silicon Effective Fano Factor from Lindhard and Dougherty

02/13/19

A.N. Villano

Abstract:

The "effective" Fano factor for nuclear recoils is something that has been studied before both theoretically [REF LIND] and experimentally [REF Dougherty]. This factor parameterizes the variance in the ionization produced from nuclear recoils, and is strongly dependent on the recoil energy of the nuclear recoils. In order to study how new data from CDMS II, SuperCDMS, and Edelweiss may constrain the variance for germanium we have to understand the previous theoretical predictions and experimental constraints for silicon. Here I show what information on the silicon predictions and measurements I've gathered and how that information is relevant to the germanium case.

7. footnotes


† There is a metastable level 73mGe at 66.7 keV which has a lifetime of 0.499 s. The reference [Isl91] lists no transition to the ground state but one to the 66.7 keV level at a strength of 1.43 %. Since that lifetime is long enough so that some of the decays will not be piled up events, we can consider a single-gamma transition with transition energy Sn - 66.7 keV. This makes the recoil energy 332.1 eV. A delayed-tag analysis might help bring out these events in data.

‡ The reference [Isl91] does not mention the transition, perhaps more modern references have constrained it. It does, however mention a continuum transition to the 595.9 keV level at a strength of 0.06 %. That level is only coupled to the ground state by the emission of an ~ 595.9 keV gamma. The transition is fast (12.41 ps) so it's likeley they're emitted near-simultaneously. Since a 595.9 keV gamma has a probability of escaping a germanium detector of ~cm scale, we can calculate a range of recoil energies depending on if the second gamma is emitted parallel or anti-parallel to the first. In the former case we get the same recoil energy as the single-gamma case, 755.1 eV, in the latter case we get 669.4 eV.

§ There is a metastable level 75mGe at 139.7 keV which has a lifetime of 47.7 s. The reference [Meier10] measures the capture on 74Ge that results in this state via a single (I think) gamma: 74Ge(n,γ)75mGe to be about 130.5 mb for thermal neutrons. This is comparable to the ground state value they measure, 497 mb. If we take the strength for the g.s. capture to be 2.83 %[Isl91] and consider the two possibilities to be mutually exclusive, then the strength for the metastable case is 0.74 %. Finding captures to this metastable state might benefit from a delayed tag on a 139.7 keV event, and the preceding nuclear recoil would be 290.4 eV. This has a benefit over looking for a trailing tag in capture to 73mGe because 75Ge isn't naturally present and is anyway unstable, so this trailing tag is not competing with inelastic excitation from neutrons (if a high-energy source like 252Cf is used).

¶ There is a metastable level 77mGe at 159.7 keV which has a lifetime of 53.7 s and decays either through isomeric transition (19 %) or beta decay (81 %). The reference [Meier12] measures the strength for capture to this state via 76Ge(n,γ)77mGe to be 25.53 %. These facts give the expected recoil an energy of 243.9 eV.
